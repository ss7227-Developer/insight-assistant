Evaluating Large Language Models: Benchmarks, Metrics, and Open Problems

Evaluating large language models (LLMs) is one of the most actively debated areas in AI research. The rapid growth in model capability has repeatedly outpaced evaluation infrastructure, with models achieving near-human or superhuman performance on benchmarks within months of those benchmarks being published, a phenomenon sometimes called benchmark saturation.

Academic benchmarks have historically dominated LLM evaluation. GLUE and its successor SuperGLUE measured performance across a suite of natural language understanding tasks including textual entailment, coreference resolution, and question answering. Both were saturated by large pre-trained models shortly after release. MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding) extended this to 57 academic subjects spanning science, law, history, and mathematics, requiring models to demonstrate breadth of world knowledge. BIG-Bench and BIG-Bench Hard further raised the difficulty bar with tasks specifically designed to resist early saturation, including logical deduction, causal reasoning, and multi-step arithmetic.

Coding and reasoning benchmarks have become central evaluation targets for frontier models. HumanEval measures the fraction of programming problems a model solves correctly when run against unit tests (pass@k). MATH evaluates multi-step mathematical reasoning over competition problems. GSM8K tests grade-school math word problems with chain-of-thought solutions. Performance on these benchmarks correlates strongly with user-perceived model usefulness for knowledge-work tasks.

Automated metrics for open-ended generation include BLEU, ROUGE, and METEOR, which measure n-gram overlap with reference answers. These metrics are fast and reproducible but poorly correlated with human judgments for long-form, creative, or reasoning-heavy outputs. BERTScore computes semantic similarity using contextual embeddings, which better captures meaning-preserving paraphrase but still struggles with factual precision.

LLM-as-judge evaluation, pioneered by the MT-Bench paper and popularized by Chatbot Arena, uses a capable LLM such as GPT-4 to rate the quality of model outputs on criteria like helpfulness, factual accuracy, and coherence. This approach scales cheaply and correlates well with human preference in A/B comparisons, but introduces evaluation bias correlated with the judge model's own style and training data.

Chatbot Arena (now LMSYS Arena) has become the most widely trusted open evaluation platform for instruction-following models. Users submit queries, receive anonymous side-by-side responses from two models, and vote for the better answer. Elo ratings derived from millions of votes provide a robust ranking that reflects real-world user preferences across a diverse distribution of tasks. Notably, rankings on Arena often differ substantially from rankings on academic benchmarks, highlighting the gap between benchmark performance and practical utility.

Safety and alignment evaluation is an increasingly critical dimension. TruthfulQA measures whether models produce factually correct answers to questions designed to elicit common misconceptions. BBQ tests models for social biases across demographic categories. Red-teaming evaluations, conducted both manually by human researchers and automatically using adversarial LLMs, probe for harmful outputs including jailbreaks, toxic content generation, and privacy violations.

Hallucination remains a central challenge and evaluation target. Models frequently generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect statements, particularly when prompted about obscure topics or asked to reason about recent events beyond their training cutoff. FActScore decomposes generated biographies into atomic facts and verifies each against a knowledge source, providing a fine-grained hallucination rate. RAGAs and ARES specifically evaluate hallucination and faithfulness in retrieval-augmented generation settings.

Emerging evaluation trends include process-reward models that score reasoning chains step by step rather than only the final answer, long-context evaluations that test retrieval of information from 100K-token documents (e.g., RULER, LongBench), and agentic benchmarks such as SWE-bench that measure whether models can autonomously resolve real GitHub issues by writing and executing code over multiple steps.

Fundamental open problems include evaluation contamination (test sets leaking into training data), the lack of ground truth for subjective or open-ended tasks, and the challenge of evaluating model behavior in rare but high-stakes scenarios that are underrepresented in any finite benchmark.
